My research investigates how users enact and shape the governance of digital platform technologies. I usually focus on communities as a valuable locus of collective organizing to anticipate and respond to potential harms that the design of platforms may expose people to, such as those around online safety, information integrity, and algorithmic bias.

My PhD advisor is the wonderful Aaron Shaw, and I am part of the Community Data Science Collective.

more about my research

  • mixed methods, primarily utilizing computational social science approaches alongside interview studies;
  • interdisciplinary: influenced by political science + government studies, sociology, human-computer interaction, science and technology studies, critical computing + data studies, and feminist theory
  • broadly motivated by questions of accountability, autonomy, the role of everyday users, scale, polycentricity, and what behavioral patterns tell us about power, participation, institutions, and good socio-technical design;
  • my dissertation revolves around how community autonomy to self-govern in different ways can bring us to more robust paradigms of platform governance. much of this was inspired by the basic observation that online groups *variably* defer to, transform, and subvert platform governance, which intuitively has implications for platform-level regulation.
